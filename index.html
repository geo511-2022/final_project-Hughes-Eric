Examining food access in Buffalo, NY, based on median household income and access to a vehicle

Eric Hughes

Introduction

Access to healthy food is not equal across all regions. Many factors influence food access such as income (Walker et al., 2010), vehicle access (Crowe et al., 2018), and proximity to quality grocery retailers (Moore & Diez Roux, 2006). The most basic measure of food access is proximity to supermarkets because supermarkets have the resources to carry a wide range of products and have affordable prices (Moore & Diez Roux, 2006; Sallis et al., 1986), making their presence a good measure of local food security. Using Moore & Diez’s (2006) definition of a supermarket, only grocery stores with 50 or more employees are considered supermarkets.

This project aims to look at Buffalo residents’ proximity to supermarkets based on their median household income and access to a vehicle. To do this, two analyses will be run. For the first, Buffalo residents will be grouped into four different income brackets based on their median household income per census tract. Then, the distance to the nearest supermarket for each census tract will be calculated. All census tracts within the same bracket will be averaged. For the second analysis, Buffalo residents will be grouped into four brackets based on the proportions of households within each census tract without access to a personal vehicle. Then, the distance to the nearest supermarket for each census tract will be calculated. All census tracts within the same bracket will be averaged. To calculate distances for each census tract, each census tract polygon will first be converted to a centroid. Results will be compared and discussed in the Results and Conclusions sections.

Materials and methods

Data sources

  • US Census Bureau will provide census tract data of median household income and vehicle access

  • Data.buffalony.gov was used for the neighborhood boundary shapefile

  • ESRI Business Analyst provided all supermarket data

# Calculate distances from centroids to supermarkets

buffalo_network <- dodgr_streetnet (pts = xy, expand = 0.2, quiet = FALSE)
graph <- weight_streetnet (buffalo_network, wt_profile = "motorcar")
graph <- graph [which (graph$component == 1), ]

to_y <- bf_supermarkets$ylat

b <- st_as_sf(data.frame(centroids, df))
names(b)[20] <- "dist"
# Create brackets
b_df <- as_data_frame(b)

# median income brackets
less_than_20k <- income_proj %>%
  filter(median.household.income <= 20000) %>%

  filter(median.household.income > 20000, median.household.income <= 40000) %>%

less_than_60k <- income_proj %>%
  filter(median.household.income > 40000, median.household.income <= 60000) %>%

Conclusions

The results for the median household income data were a bit surprising. Given that supermarkets tend to favor more affluent neighborhoods, expectations were that as each income bracket increased in wealth, the distance would decrease. While the highest earners had to travel the least distance, the other three brackets did not show a linear progression of decreasing distances as income increased. However, the vehicle access data yielded the expected results. As the proportion of households’ access to vehicles decreased, the average distance to supermarkets increased. This is expected because people without access to a vehicle typically come from vulnerable communities where supermarkets are less inclined to enter.

Further research could be done by examining demographic variables such as race and education. By looking into these variables, further insights could be gained to further explain food access and the distribution of supermarkets in Buffalo, NY.
